Project overview

What is #ManyDesignsCarbon?

#ManyDesignsCarbon is a crowd-science project on whether behavioral interventions can increase public support for carbon pricing. The main results shown here summarize 55 independently designed studies run under a shared protocol with 19,558 U.S. participants.

Why many designs?

A single randomized study is essentially one draw from a noisy distribution and only tells us whether an intervention works in one specific setting. Its scope is therefore limited, and with typical sample sizes it often has limited statistical power. A many-designs approach asks a broader question: what happens when many independent research teams design different interventions for the same policy problem, all evaluated under a shared framework (omparable measures, same test population, and a unified analysis plan)? This leverages the wisdom of an expert crowd and offers several advantages. First, it strongly increases effective sample size and thus statistical power at the aggregate level. Second, it makes design heterogeneity visible: instead of a single estimate, it yields a distribution of effects and shows how sensitive results are to design choices. Third, it enables immediate evidence aggregation using established methods (e.g., random-effects meta-analysis), rather than waiting for years of accumulated studies, and also aids in investigating what exactly makes interventions work by examining highly comparable studies together. Fourth, it reduces the risk that conclusions hinge on a single, potentially idiosyncratic design, improving robustness and external validity. Finally, because all designs are preregistered, all materials are shared online and the data is analyzed in a coordinated manner, and reported regardless of results, transparency, reproducibility, and replicability are enhanced, while publication bias is reduced.

How designs entered the study and were implemented

Research teams applied to participate and were randomly selected from a large international applicant pool. Selected teams then submitted pre-registered design proposals following a common protocol. Each study was required to include both a treatment and a control condition, and to implement a consequential, real-world measure of support for carbon pricing (e.g., donations, petitions, or political engagement). In addition to designing and programming their own experiments, teams also provided ex-ante predictions of their expected effect sizes. To further capture expert judgment, teams were randomly assigned to evaluate a subset of other designs, generating independent peer assessments alongside self-assessments. All studies were then implemented simultaneously on a shared participant pool under a unified analysis framework, ensuring comparability across designs.

What the project learned

On average, behavioral interventions produced small but statistically detectable increases in real-world and stated support for carbon pricing, roughly around two percentage points across the main measures. At the same time, effects differed across designs, and the prediction intervals show that future interventions from the same design space could plausibly have null or negative effects.

Open science

The project was pre-registered, received ethics approval from the University of Innsbruck, and provides the pre-registered study protocol, data, and analysis files in the OSF repository.
